The Cost of Eating Local

Course: ENPH 110 Intro to Environmental Health

Authors: Seth Vosters, David Van SlamBrouck, Emily Goeltl

Comparing the prices of fast food versus the cost of supermarket food and local food is important because it helps us decide where we should spend our money and why. This topic is especially important because it involves agriculture and transportation of food which both have a large impact on the environment. This topic is interesting because it allows us to examine how the convenience of fast food can outweigh the benefits of a well-planned diet with foods from supermarket chains. Our goal in this project (website) is to increase our understanding of why foods are priced by researching how the food gets to fast food restaurants, supermarkets and local foods. We hope that by creating this website that we can easily figure out the opportunity cost of buying food from different sources.

Price Comparisions

The standard held conception is that eating local and organic is more expensive and often we do little to quest that narrative. We decided to actually visit the source and do a side by side comparison where we could to determine the validity of said conception. We started off by visiting Just Local Food. There I spoke with Cody Maciosek and Mattie Kahn.

Before getting to that discussion we should discuss some other critical factors to this topic. Foremost amongst these is our local hardiness zone of 4b per the USDA’s website on growing zones (USDA, 2012) . This means that by using conventional farming methods the typical growing season is limited by frost dates. This typically means that the season is restricted to roughly the range of May 6th – October 2nd according to the Farmer’s Almanac website (Yankee Publishing, Inc., 2017). Because of this the variety of crops and amount are limited to what can be grown within this timeframe.

With that being said we can move back to our interview with Cody Macoisek and Mattie Kahn. Just Local Food is a Co-op and as such is dependent on suppliers to the co-op (Maciosek, 2018) . During the growing season of their products will be that provided by the area producers. However, as a necessity during the winter they function much like a traditional grocery store for produce (Kahn, 2018). With that being said we can’t compare prices on produce because for the most part it simply isn’t available at the time of writing (1.15.18). However, I will say from personal experience the Farmer’s Market prices during the season are typically lower than those of comparable products at the major local grocers. So, without a further ado lets look at some examples of products where we can draw a price comparison. For these comparisons we will use the local Woodmans as our representation of the major grocery chains in our area.

Prices for Just Local Food will be displayed on the left while prices for Woodman's will be displayed on the right just like the pictures above.

Eggs

Here we see Woodman’s is significantly higher in quantity and lower in price.

Milk

Here again we see that Woodman’s is ahead in the price category.

Lettuce

This is one of the few produce items comparable due to non-traditional hydroponic growing in our area. Yet still we see a price lead by Woodman’s here as well.

Whole Chicken

In this case we see the same price is available at both locations.

Pork Chops

Again, Woodman’s ahead by a fair amount per pound.

Ground Beef

Again, we see Woodman’s with the lower price point.

Now in these seven specific examples we see that prices typically favor the major grocers. From a purely logical standpoint that makes sense on just a measure of scale. Because the local grocers have higher volume they can afford lower margins on products and still turn a profit.

How does fast food compare? While this is certainly a case of comparing Apples to Oranges in a sense that we are looking at ingredients vs. a finished product we can still do a quick glance to see if anything stands out.

(Culver's Menu and Prices, 2018)

Perhaps the easiest comparison can be drawn on chicken prices. Shopping at Just Local Foods we could purchase two whole chickens for the price of the smallest Chicken To Go order from Culvers and end up with vastly more food making Just Local Foods a clear winner there without even considering the added health benefits of avoiding fast food.

Seeing that, we can say that yes, the typical convention that eating local food does have a higher price point does hold true in the specific cases that we looked at today, but that should be taken with a number of caveats.

  1. The price may be higher, but not prohibitory so. Pork Chops for example, even at 6.99/lb. is only .67cents per serving more when you get 3 chops per pound. Meaning for a family of four which ate pork chops once a week for a month would cost only an additional $10.72 a month.
  2. We are comparing during the off season which is a distinct disadvantage to local food options
  3. These seven items might not be indicative of what you actually eat and thus are not a good representation of what your cost comparison might be.
  4. We are not being ultra specific with comparisions here. We didn't measure items such as pork chop width, ground beef fat content percentage or gross weights of similar sized salad containers.

While the prices are certainly lower at major grocery chains, there are certainly other benifits associated with with eating local that work to ofset some of that price difference.

  • You have access to ask how a product was produced
  • You can support someone local and keep money in the community
  • You can determine which pesticides are used
  • You can ask how animals are treated in the production of a given product

You simply do not have that type of access at a major grocery chain. Looking back at the interview with Mr. Maciosek and Mr. Kahn, I asked who the major demographic of the store was, while they stated that many different groups were present, the vast majority of sales came from those who are more affluent (Maciosek, 2018) . I think that is an interesting fact that should not be over looked. When purchasing or spending power is not a concern these folks choose to shop local and support the farmers and local grower’s movement in our community. Perhaps in doing so the will allow said farmers or others to eventually increase in scale or technology and drag the prices down while still maintaining the other positive environmental benefits of the movement.
